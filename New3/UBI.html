UNIVERSAL BASIC INCOME AND UNEMPLOYMENT ALLOWANCE

UNIVERSAL BASIC INCOME AND UNEMPLOYMENT ALLOWANCE


PUBLIC POLICY SUBMISSION: AAP INTERNSHIP
Aradhya Shandilya | 5th June, 2018

Introductory Note

Universal Basic Income, or as many call it an ‘unconditional transfer’, is in simpler words a social security or the money you’re entitled to. Doesn’t matter to the state –rich or poor, employed or unemployed. The Economic survey puts it as a conceptually appealing idea but fiscally, unaffordable. Whereas, the Unemployment Allowance is the money that the state provides to the unemployed population. A large number of diverse school of thoughts lie on two opposite ends of a political and an economic spectra on this matter and this report is an attempt to analyse those with the help of some questions put forth.

Governance Areas:

The central schemes are divided into two categories- the central sector schemes and the centrally sponsored schemes. While the central sector schemes revolve around the union list and receive a 100% funding by the centre, the centrally sponsored schemes function a bit differently with certain contribution from the states as well. According to the Economic Survey, based on a survey on misallocation of resources for the six largest Central Sector and Centrally Sponsored Sub-Schemes (except PDS and fertilizer subsidy) across districts, the report points out that the districts where the needs are greatest are precisely the ones where State capacity is the weakest. This suggests that a more efficient way to help the poor would be to provide them resources directly, through a UBI. For which, exploring the principles and prerequisites for successful implementation of UBI, the Survey points out that the two prerequisites for a successful UBI are: (a) functional JAM (Jan Dhan, Aadhar and Mobile) system as it ensures that the cash transfer goes directly into the account of a beneficiary and (b) Centre-State negotiations on cost sharing for the programme like the centrally sponsored subschemes. According to the Economic Survey of 2015 the Jan Dhan Yojana has the power to remove ‘every tear from every eye’. However, 4 years after its implementation even though we hear news like JDY helped increase the total number of people with a bank account tremendously but what needs to be understood here is that 48% of these accounts opened after the implementation of the Jan Dhan Yojana have had no activity in these years-neither deposit nor withdrawal. To assure the ‘financial inclusion’ that was one of the few goals of this yojana, we need to make sure that there is availability of financial credit to these people and the fact that they have savings in financial institutions.

Cause-Effect analysis:

The Relation with Unemployment and Poverty in India: With approx. 50 welfare schemes and poverty alleviation programmes running in the country, the nation still has an unemployed population of a large 20M and over 200M people falling under the poverty line. The matter of conern over here is that this poverty data estimate too has been calculated according to the recommendations of the Tendulkar Committee and not the Rangarjan committee which takes into account the non-food expenditures of the family as well and hence states Rs. 1407, Rs. 972 (for urban, rural population respectively) to be the amount taken into consideration while associating with the poverty line and not Rs. 1000, Rs. 816 (for urban, rural population respectively) for as recommended by the Tendulkar committee. According to the economic Survey, UBI reduces poverty to 0.5% of population and would cost 4-5% of GDP, assuming that those in top 25% income bracket do not participate. The existing middleclass, food, petroleum and fertilisers subsidies cost about 3% of GDP.

Lack of experimental data:

A greater depth of pilot studies, focussed on ensuring universal access and covering minimum living expenses is required. What needs to be understood is that what worked in Kenya or Manitoba might not work in India because of a lot of contributing factors.

However, A pilot in eight villages in Madhya Pradesh provided over 6,000 individuals a monthly payment (Rs. 100 for a child, Rs. 200 for an adult; later raised to Rs. 150 and Rs. 300, respectively). The money was initially paid out as cash, while transitioning to bank accounts three months later. The results were intriguing. Most villagers used the money on household improvements (latrines, walls, roofs) while taking precautions against malaria — 24.3 per cent of the households changed their main source of energy for cooking or lighting; 16 per cent had made changes to their toilet. There was a seeming shift towards markets, instead of ration shops, given better financial liquidity, leading to improved nutrition, particularly among SC and ST households, and better school attendance and performance. There was an increase in small-scale investments (better seeds, sewing machines, equipment repairs etc). Bonded labour decreased, along with casual wage labour, while self-employed farming and business activity increased. Financial inclusion was rapid – within four months of the pilot, 95.6 per cent of the individuals had bank accounts. Within a year, 73 per cent of the households reported a reduction in their debt. There was no evidence of any increase in spending on alcohol.

Comparative Analysis:

Right-wing economists even though argue that after providing UBI, the state need not increase its expenditure on education and health by another 6% to 8% of GDP to reach the benchmark set by certain advanced developing economies. However, the ones on the centre and left say that the state must continue to fulfil its obligation even after providing UBI. These economists believe that the UBI must essentially operate like a social security payment in India.

Now, certain conservative economists also say that this must be done without unduly expanding the fisc. The economists argue that it can be done partly by collapsing the funds allocated for MNREGA and food security into UBI. However, the fact of matter is that this will not work simply because the PDS system works very well in the southern part of the country like Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Orissa and the historic evidences tell us that these states might not readily allign with the centre’s demands and thus these states might not agree to dismantle them..

Kela, the national social-insurance institute of the government of Finland randomly selected 2,000 Finns between 25 and 58 years of age who were already getting some form of the unemployment benefits to receive a flat monthly amount of €560 (about $685) for two years. Unemployed people in Finland are already eligible for a benefits package roughly equal in value to the basic income payment. The difference in this two-year program is that people would still receive the basic-income stipend even if they secured a job, while traditional unemployment benefits disappear once someone starts working.

Recommendations and Alternatives:

There mainly exists two opinions on the implementation of the Universal Basic Income-one being the elimination of all welfare programmes and the other being to not see UBI as a substitute for the public provision of services.

Funds:

Green box subsidies, i.e. subsidies that have no trade intervention or price support, like research infrastructure, farmer training, disaster aid and so on must be promoted. This makes the food security program more self sufficient and removes burden from the exchequer. Notably, India’s reliance on amber box subsidies which include price subsidy at both the input and output ends has invited friction at the WTO

5.2 Examining the Scope of Co-operatives and SHGs

With the Nation’s Tax to GDP ratio being as low as 17.7, many believe that high taxes could be an answer to the need of resouces for the UBI. The real estate and property tax assesments are absurdly low when compared to their market value. The zero taxation of agricultural income, long term capital gains in equity markets, of wealth and inheritance-if fixed can help allocate funds. ● Terminating wasteful welfare programmes which in practice are not yielding the results they were/are expected to.

5.3 Promotion of Decentralized Procurement

Instead of producing for the food security program across the country, logistical costs can be trimmed by localizing production. This makes the functioning of the program hassle- free and reduces financial and administrative burden.

5.4 Storage Infrastructure

Owing to an unstable supply and a weather sensitive produce, it is highly important to create and store a buffer stock in times of adversities to cushion the supply and price fluctuation due to adverse factors. Investments on cold-storages and warehouses hold the key.

For identifying the beneficiaries, a possible solution could be to either use the JAM and if we cannot identify the top 25% of the population then, to provide the Universal Basic Income and the Unempployment allowance to everyone and by default the people having high incomes would end up paying more taxes hence rendering their net govt provided income less.

6. Eradicating Hunger: A Five-Year Approach

The strides towards complete eradication of hunger can be boosted by means of a well laid out five year plan. Even though deemed to be an ambitious approach, it holds promise and the results would be purely subjected to the beforehand research and implementation. Since the basic aim is to eradicate poverty and provide employment opportunities, a similar model such as the MGNREGA, should be put into force in the urban areas as well. Thus, the Ministry of Urban Development, Rural Development and Finance Ministry should work together to in this regard to increase employment opportunities in urban areas as well.

Executive summary:

Beginning with a definition of both the terms involved, the report traverses via answering various questions and putting forth various schools of opinion and a comparative analysis between the opinions of various economists. The report answers crucial questions like who should be implementing the Universal Basic Income, who the target audience shall be and about various government related schemes and government data. The report ends with its recommendations and possible solutions..
